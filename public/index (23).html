Mont-Saint-Michel Abbey Tickets: Prices, Hours & What's Inside

The abbey is the reason Mont-Saint-Michel exists — and the one part most visitors pay for. Here's how tickets work in 2026, and what waits at the top.

Abbey tickets at a glance

The abbey is ticketed — the village is free, but the abbey needs a ticket, and there are reduced and free categories for young people and EU residents. Buy online in advance with a fixed time slot, especially in high season: the on-site queue can run over an hour, and daily visitor numbers are capped. Opening hours run from morning to early evening, longer in summer, plus a few days a year when the abbey is closed.

Abbey tickets
The medieval village is free for everyone. A ticket is needed only for the abbey. From 1 April to 30 September 2026 the adult ticket is €16; from 1 October to 31 March it's €13. Free for under-18s, and for 18–25-year-olds who are EU citizens or residents.

How the visit actually works

The abbey is a Benedictine monastery that climbs the rock in layers. You don't move from one room to the next on a single floor — you follow a one-way route that rises and falls through the church, the cloister, the refectory and the halls built into the north slope. It's a visit with a lot of stairs: plan for that, and wear proper shoes.

The route is mostly self-guided. You can do it on your own, add your own abbey audio guide, or join a guided tour. In peak season the abbey manages the flow with timed entry — which is exactly why booking ahead makes sense.

Mont-Saint-Michel Abbey tickets: types and prices

There are a few options, and the right choice saves you time and money. Standard entry is the base; there's a small add-on for the abbey's own audio guide; guided tours cost more and have limited places; and reduced or free entry applies to certain groups — as a rule, under-18s and EU residents under 26 go free. If you qualify for a concession, bring your ID.

The most useful tip: in July and August the on-site queue can eat 1.5–2 hours of your day, and the daily quota is limited — so a timed online ticket is the simplest way to skip the wait and guarantee entry. One thing many people trip on: the abbey ticket does not include the mainland car park or shuttle — those are paid separately.

Abbey opening hours

Hours change with the season — longer in summer, shorter in winter — and the last entry is usually about an hour before closing. On a few public holidays the abbey is closed. Because this shifts from year to year, check the current schedule on the official site before you travel. As a planning rule: summer hours are longer, sometimes with special evening sessions of music and light; winter closes earlier; and always look at the last-entry time, not just the closing time.

What's inside: room by room

The one-way route takes you past the main highlights in order. At the top is the abbey church: a Romanesque nave meets a later Flamboyant Gothic choir; step out onto the West Terrace in front of it for one of the widest views over the bay. The architectural jewel is La Merveille ("the Marvel"): a three-storey Gothic complex on the north slope, with a storeroom and almonry for the poor at the bottom, the Hall of the Knights and the guest hall in the middle, and the refectory and cloister at the top.

The cloister is the abbey's most photographed interior: a garden ringed by slender double columns, seemingly hung between sea and sky. The neighbouring refectory is lit by tall, narrow windows you barely notice until you're inside — a deliberate play of light. Lower down you pass the Crypt of the Large Pillars, whose massive columns hold up the choir above, and the big treadwheel from the years the abbey served as a prison, once used to haul loads up the rock. Don't leave without the view from the West Terrace, the cloister, and a look up at the gilded statue of Saint Michael crowning the spire.

Guide, audio guide, or on your own?

The format of your visit changes the experience more than people expect. On your own is the most flexible and the cheapest option beyond the ticket: you set the pace and linger where you like, though without some context read up beforehand it can feel thin. The abbey's own audio guide adds that context room by room for a small add-on, and for most independent visitors it's the sweet spot. A live guided tour costs more and ties you to a group and a schedule, but a good guide brings the empty stone halls to life with details you'd otherwise walk past. If you're choosing, go by temperament: go solo if you value freedom; take audio if you want the story without a group; take a guide if you take things in better from a person. Either way, remember: an abbey guide only covers the interior — the village and walls outside are a separate, free walk.

Island walk vs abbey visit

Best time of day to visit

Go up for the first entry in the morning — the quietest hour before the day-trip coaches arrive — or late in the day, when the buses leave and the light on the terrace softens. Midday in summer is the busiest, slowest window, best avoided. For the seasonal picture, see our guide to the best time to visit.

Accessibility

The abbey is a medieval building on a rock: lots of stairs and steep, uneven stretches, and step-free access to the upper levels is limited. Visitors with reduced mobility should check the current accessibility conditions and assistance options in advance.

Practical tips

  • In high season, buy your ticket online — skip the queue and guarantee entry against the daily cap.
  • Arrive for the first entry or late in the day for the calmest visit.
  • Wear proper shoes — the route is all stairs and worn stone.
  • Allow 1.5–2 hours inside, more with an audio guide.
  • Bring a warm layer: the halls are cool and the terrace is windy.
  • Check the last-entry time, not just the closing time.

Common mistakes

The usual slip-ups: turning up at midday in summer with no booking and losing hours in the queue; assuming free entry to the village includes the abbey interior (it doesn't); missing the last entry, roughly an hour before closing; rushing the cloister and terrace — the very things people most regret skipping; and confusing the abbey's own audio guide with a self-guided island audio walk — they're different products.

Frequently asked questions

How much are Mont-Saint-Michel Abbey tickets?

The standard adult ticket is €16 in summer and €13 in winter, with reduced and free categories for young people and EU residents.

Do I need to book tickets in advance?

In summer, yes: a timed online ticket saves a long queue and guarantees entry against the daily cap. In quieter months you can usually buy at the door.

What are the abbey's opening hours?

9:00–19:00 from May to August, and 9:30–18:00 from September to April. Last entry is one hour before closing; closed 1 January, 1 May and 25 December.

How long does an abbey visit take?

About 1.5–2 hours at a relaxed pace; a little longer with an audio guide.

Is the abbey the same as the village?

No. The walled village and ramparts are free; the abbey at the summit is a separate paid visit.

Can I visit the abbey on my own?

Yes, the route is self-guided. You can add an audio guide or join a guided tour if you like.

Is the abbey wheelchair accessible?

Access is limited by the medieval building and its many stairs; check current conditions in advance.
